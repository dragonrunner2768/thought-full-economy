In the same month that Jack Dorsey announced he was reducing Block's workforce by forty percent and calling it an AI transformation, Aaron Levie — CEO of Box, the cloud content management company — was saying something that received a fraction of the attention and deserved considerably more of it.

Levie's argument, stated clearly and repeatedly across multiple interviews, is simple enough to fit in a single question: If AI can make your team ten times more productive, why would you make the team smaller? Why not make the company ten times larger instead?

No formula. No perfectly round number backed by no methodology. Just a question — the kind of question that sounds obvious once someone asks it, and that apparently nobody in the boardrooms celebrating Dorsey's announcement thought to raise.

"The better strategy isn't reducing humans by 10x in your company. It's growing your company. If AI can increase productivity by 10x, go from 1,000 to 10,000 — without having to actually hire 9,000 people."

— Aaron Levie, Box CEO
The Specific Mechanism

The Lawyer Who Doesn't Get Fired — She Gets More Clients

Levie uses lawyers as his illustration, and it's worth following the example precisely because it demonstrates where the subtraction logic breaks down.

Imagine AI tools allow your legal team to review contracts twice as fast. The subtraction logic — Dorsey's logic — says: cut the legal team in half. Same work gets done. Payroll drops. Efficiency achieved.

Levie's logic says: don't cut anyone. Run the process twice as fast. Respond to customers in half the time. Remove the legal bottleneck that was slowing down your sales cycle. Close deals faster. Grow revenue. And then — when that revenue growth creates more legal complexity than your existing team can handle — hire more lawyers.

The same number of lawyers, operating with AI, produced more output, served more clients, removed a business bottleneck, and generated the growth that justified hiring more people. The subtraction model extracted a one-time cost reduction. The multiplication model created a compounding growth engine.

These are not two versions of the same strategy. They are opposite theories of what productivity is for.

The 161-Year-Old Law That Explains Everything

The Jevons Paradox — and Why Efficiency Expands Markets

In 1865, the English economist William Stanley Jevons observed something counterintuitive about steam engines: as they became more efficient, coal consumption didn't fall. It rose. More efficient engines made coal-powered production cheaper, which expanded the range of things it was economical to produce, which created new industries, which consumed more coal than the inefficient old engines ever had.

Jevons documented what became an economic law: efficiency gains in a resource don't reduce its consumption. They expand the market for it. The resource becomes so much more useful, at so much lower a cost per unit, that entirely new applications become viable — applications that didn't exist when the resource was expensive.

Levie is applying the Jevons paradox to human labor. If AI makes human output ten times cheaper to produce, the rational response is not to buy less of it. It is to find ten times more things worth doing with it. The market for Thought-Full human work doesn't shrink when it becomes more productive. It expands — because things that were previously too expensive to attempt become achievable, and things that were previously impossible become the new competitive frontier.

Dorsey's formula treats human labor as a cost to minimize. Levie's framework treats it as a capability to multiply. Jevons, writing 161 years ago about coal, already knew which one was right.

The Role That Survives

From Individual Contributor to Strategic Director

Levie's framework also clarifies something the fear-of-replacement narrative consistently obscures: what the human role actually becomes in an AI-augmented workplace.

In Levie's view, the individual contributor's role is beginning to resemble that of a manager — not a manager of people, but a manager of capability. Someone who understands what needs to be accomplished, allocates the right tools to the right tasks, validates the outputs, and exercises judgment on the questions the tools can't answer. The surgeon with intraoperative AI. The attorney with contract review tools. The engineer with diagnostic systems. The analyst with pattern-recognition software. In each case the human doesn't disappear — she moves up the value chain, into the layer where the stakes are higher, the decisions are harder, and the judgment required is precisely the kind that no algorithm currently produces.

This is the Thought-Full work the series argues is irreplaceable. And it is worth noting that Levie isn't making this argument as a philosophical position. He is making it as a CEO who is building Box's entire product strategy around the assumption that human judgment is the premium layer in every AI-augmented workflow. His business model depends on being right about this. Dorsey's business model, on the other hand, depended on the formula being right — and the formula has no peer review.

The Risk
Innovation deficit. Competitors inherit the talent. The next Cash App doesn't get built. Execution risk. Multiplication requires humans who are genuinely Thought-Full. AI amplifies mediocrity as readily as it amplifies excellence.
What It Says About Humans
Interchangeable with machines at sufficient AI capability. A liability to be managed. The irreplaceable layer above the machine. An asset to be compounded.

It is worth noting that the multiplication model carries its own risk, and intellectual honesty requires naming it: AI amplifies whatever the humans directing it bring to the work. If those humans are genuinely Thought-Full — curious, rigorous, accountable, willing to interrogate their own assumptions — AI makes them exponentially more valuable. If they are not, AI makes their mediocrity faster, cheaper, and more difficult to identify until the damage is done. The Jevons paradox expands markets for excellence and for carelessness with equal enthusiasm.

This is why the multiplication model is not simply a more optimistic version of Dorsey's formula. It is a more demanding one. It requires the humans in the loop to actually be worth multiplying.

The Implication

What This Means for Susan

Return to the nineteen-year-old at the beginning of this series, laptop open, reading article after article about AI displacement. The question she is actually trying to answer is not whether AI will change her career. It will. The question is what kind of human she needs to become to be worth multiplying rather than worth replacing.

Levie's framework answers that more precisely than any comfort she will find in generic reassurance. The humans who thrive in the multiplication model are the ones who develop genuine judgment — the capacity to allocate tools, validate outputs, ask the questions the algorithm can't form, and take responsibility for the decisions that matter. That is not a credential. It is a practiced capability, built deliberately, over time, starting now.
