Editorial Note — This article is a speculative retrospective: a journalistic exercise written in 2026 as if from 2029, using the real events of Block's February 2026 layoff announcement as its foundation. All references to post-2026 events, figures, and outcomes are fictional projections designed to illustrate a real strategic argument. The events of February 2026 — the layoffs, the Dorsey formula, the stock surge — are documented fact.

Three years ago, in February 2026, Jack Dorsey — then CEO of Block — stated an astonishing business labor formula: 100 people plus AI equals 1,000 people. Wall Street celebrated. Boardrooms salivated. And workforces trembled in fear. What appeared to be a brilliant way forward unfortunately masked a trove of problems. What Dorsey has spent the last three years proving is that AI can't solve problems — but it's pretty good at automating them.

Before we go further, let's acknowledge what was actually true in February 2026: Block had overhired through the pandemic years, as had most of the tech industry. The financial correction was real and justified. Most companies making similar moves called it what it was — a post-pandemic restructuring, painful but honest. Dorsey called it AI. And that choice of framing deserves more scrutiny than it received.

Start with the formula itself. Real quantitative models — the kind that get stress-tested by economists, reviewed by independent analysts, and stress-tested against historical data — do not produce round numbers. They produce results like 65 people equals 847 productivity units under specific operational conditions, with a confidence interval and a list of assumptions attached. Messy numbers, because reality is messy. A clean 10x multiplier — exactly 100 becoming exactly 1,000 — is not the output of rigorous analysis. It is a talking point. It is a number engineered to survive a news cycle, not a peer review. No methodology was published. No independent economists vetted it. No confidence intervals were offered. The formula appeared, fully formed, in a company-wide memo, attributed to a man whose most celebrated creation — Twitter — was a genuinely revolutionary platform that was, for most of its existence, famously and chronically unprofitable.

This matters. Dorsey is a visionary product thinker. He is not a quantitative economist and has never claimed to be. But a formula that purports to describe the future productivity of thousands of human workers deserves the rigor we would apply to any other claim of that magnitude. Nobody in that boardroom apparently asked: how was this derived? What does the data show? What are the conditions under which 100 becomes 1,000 rather than 100 becoming 200? The questions were not asked. The formula was not tested. The layoffs were announced. The stock surged 24 percent in a single afternoon. And a very specific group of people — the ones holding equity, not the ones holding severance checks — had an exceptionally good day.

Time will vet the formula. It always does. In the meantime, the investors got their bonus. The question the next three years will answer is who paid for it.

"AI can't solve problems. But it's pretty good at automating them."

The Window Opens

What Everyone Else Did While Block Was Optimizing

The hiring that followed was aggressive, targeted, and, from the outside, almost surgical. Stripe, Chime, Klarna, and a cluster of well-funded startups didn't recruit generically. They recruited specifically — former Block product leads, engineers who had built Cash App's core infrastructure, designers who knew Square's merchant product from the inside. The pitch was simple: bring your ideas, we'll build them.

Aggressive US expansion through 2027–2028 targeted Afterpay's merchant relationships directly, exploiting a product development gap Block's reduced team could not close fast enough.

Relay Financial's story is the one that stings most. At the 2027 CES Convention in Las Vegas, its CEO — a former Block product manager — was asked how the company had moved so quickly from founding to a product that was already threatening its former employer. His answer was unscripted and has since been quoted in half a dozen business school case studies:

"It was a no-brainer. All those engineers sitting around the cafeteria saying 'if only' — if only we had the runway to build this, if only leadership would greenlight that. Well. Here we are, with all those if-only products. Turns out the only thing standing between an idea and a company was someone telling us we no longer had jobs."

— CEO, Relay Financial  ·  CES Convention, Las Vegas, January 2027

Relay went public last month. Its market cap on day one exceeded what Block's stock lost in the eighteen months following the layoff announcement.

The Closing Thought

The Formula Had a Variable Nobody Counted

Dorsey's equation — 100 people plus AI equals 1,000 people — was not wrong about what AI can do. It was wrong about what it was replacing. AI can make engineers faster. It cannot make them curious. It can optimize a product. It cannot imagine the next one. The variable the formula left out was judgment — the accumulated, experience-built, friction-tested human capacity to look at a problem nobody has named yet and decide it's worth solving.

Block didn't automate its way to a better company. It automated its way to a smaller one — and handed the difference to nine startups and every competitor in fintech who was paying attention. Bad ideas, it turns out, don't get better with automation. They just arrive at their consequences faster.
